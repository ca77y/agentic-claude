# Epic Template

Use for large, uncertain, or multi-story work. An epic frames the work area and provides enough researched context to design stories later. Do not create child stories in the same pass.

```markdown
## Goal

[What this larger body of work should achieve and why it matters.]

## Success Signals

- [Observable product, user, business, operational, or quality signal that indicates progress]
- [Optional metric, leading indicator, or qualitative outcome]

## Background

[Research findings, product context, domain context, and why this is worth grouping as an epic.]

## Problem Space

[The user/business/domain problem this epic covers.]

## Current State

[How the product, flow, system, or process works today.]

## Desired Future State

[What should be true after the epic is substantially delivered.]

## Scope

[What belongs inside the epic.]

## Out of Scope

[Boundaries and non-goals.]

## Constraints

- [Product, UX, technical, data, privacy, provider, performance, or compatibility constraints]
- [Existing behavior, contracts, or workflows that must not regress across child stories]

## Dependencies

- [Related epics, Linear issues, systems, providers, migrations, design decisions, or docs needed before/during child stories]

## Risks

- [Likely delivery, product, technical, migration, rollout, or adoption risks]

## Candidate Stories

- [Possible future story, not created yet; include sequencing hint if useful]
- [Possible future story, not created yet; include sequencing hint if useful]

## References

- [docs/library/... or other docs/code/Linear/web references needed for planning]

## Verification Strategy

- [How child stories should prove progress: tests, analytics, QA checks, runtime validation, or review gates]

## Open Questions

- [Question that should be resolved before or while designing child stories]
```

Keep the artifact at outcome and problem-space level. Candidate stories are notes for future analyst passes, not created work.
